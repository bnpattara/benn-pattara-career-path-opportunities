Point of View

Gap Inc. has something no DTC brand can replicate: 40 million members and 1,400 stores. The question is whether those two assets talk to each other. Right now, most lifecycle programs treat in-store behavior and digital engagement as separate signals. A customer who redeems in-store, returns a week later, and never opens a push notification is invisible to a system that only reads clicks. That is not a data problem. It is an architecture problem.

The brands that win the next decade of loyalty will be the ones that treat every touchpoint as part of one continuous conversation with a real person. Gap has the scale to do that. What it needs is the strategic layer to close the loop between what happens on the floor and what gets sent to your inbox.

01
Lifecycle Begins at Enrollment
Most programs treat acquisition and retention as sequential. They are not. The conversation you have at card enrollment sets the entire relationship. If the first touchpoint is transactional, the whole lifecycle will be. The fix is not a better welcome email. It is a different enrollment experience.
02
In-Store Is an Underused Signal
A customer who visits twice in one month, tries on three things, and buys one is telling you something. That behavior is currently invisible to most digital lifecycle systems. The physical network is not a cost center to be managed. It is a data source that DTC competitors would pay for. The program that learns to read it first has a durable advantage.
03
Loyalty Is Earned After the Sale
Getting someone into the program is half the job. What happens in the 90 days after enrollment determines whether they stay. Most programs go quiet after activation. The ones that win send something that matters: a reward tied to a behavior, a reminder that feels like it knows you, a reason to come back that is not a discount. That is the lifecycle problem worth solving.
